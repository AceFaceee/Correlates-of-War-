What is COW?
The Correlates of War (COW) project aims to enhance the accessibility and reliability of quantitative data in the field of international relations. The project is committed to non-profit sharing data sets with the research community, providing timely data releases, versioning for tracking, comprehensive dataset documentation, and continuous efforts to update and distribute improved dataset versions. The project utilizes its website as a central platform for data distribution, error reporting, user interaction, and community engagement to advance the project's development within the realm of international relations.



Who are in charge of the datasets?
According to COW websites, Associate Directors (Bennett) is in charge of supervising and managing the datasets. Advisory Boards (D'Orazio et al) is in charge of designing, structring, and encoding of datasets. Finally, the Data Hosts (Gibler et al): who are in charge of their hosting dataset (one by each person) and maintains the correctness and completeness of such.



Commercial or profit-driven usage of the site is **forbidden**.

**_What has been updated?_**
**I. Military data update:** In 2017, updates to MILPER data (Military Personnel) were implemented for NMC v5.0, enhancing accuracy and detail, particularly for the 2002-2007 and 2008-2012 periods. These revisions included recalculating specific MILPER values and improving source information, providing a more refined and informative dataset compared to the previous NMC v4.0 from 2010.
**II.Population data update:** The 2017 update of total population data followed the methodology from the 2010 (4.0) update, involving a comprehensive revision of total population figures for the 2002-2007 period and an extension for 2008-2012 to enhance data consistency. The update primarily sourced data from the online version of the UN World Urbanization Prospects, 2014 Revision, with exceptions for Kosovo and Taiwan where data was derived from the World Bank and National Statistics Republic of China Social Indicators reports, respectively, ensuring accurate and consistent data without requiring interpolation or extrapolation for the 2002-2012 period.
**III. Steel update:**The World Steel Association’s Steel Statistical Yearbook 2013 (Table 1, “Total Production of Crude Steel”) was employed to update IRST for the period 2008-2012.
**IV. Population updates:** Starting in 2002, a transition in how urban population data is generated was initiated, aiming to shift from urban population to urban agglomeration as a measure. The 2017 update significantly improved access to comprehensive urban agglomeration data, addressing limitations in the previous UN Demographic Yearbook source. Also, political regions like Taiwan, Kesovo were reclassfied.
There are also other updates on variety of details, mainly, more up-to-date, more accurate, more complete compared to v4.

Identification problems
1. Definition and Operationalization of Variables: Many political, military concepts are hard to quantify. The paper use proxy variable for politcal, religous, and military status for a country. This may cause a operationalization error, that is, there is a difference between proxies and reality.
2. Missing Data: The data encoded -9 is missing, which may cause information incompleteness.
3. Aggregation Level Mismatch (improper integration): Aggregating data at different levels (e.g., country-level, regional-level) may obscure important variations and nuances, affecting causal inference. Also, some regions with geo-political controversies may have caveats with data.
4. Heterogeneity of Units: Units (e.g., countries) may vary significantly in terms of size, population, geopolitical influence, etc., making it challenging to treat them uniformly in causal analysis.

Variables correlated by nature?
1. Tpop (Total Population) and Upop (Urban Population):
It's likely that total population (tpop) and urban population (upop) are correlated. As a country's overall population increases, its urban population is likely to increase proportionally due to factors such as urbanization and migration to cities.
2. Tpop (Total Population) and Pec (Primary Energy Consumption):
Total population (tpop) and primary energy consumption (pec) may be correlated. Larger populations generally require more energy for various purposes, including household and industrial needs.
3. Upop (Urban Population) and Pec (Primary Energy Consumption):
Urban population (upop) and primary energy consumption (pec) might be correlated as well. Urban areas tend to have higher energy needs due to concentrated economic activities, transportation systems, and residential consumption.
4. Milex (Military Expenditures) and Milper (Military Personnel):
Military Expenditures and personnel has simultaniety relationship. As more military personnel requires more budget to train, accomodate, and administrate. Also, more military expenditures spent on facilities and crafts require expertise personnel to take care.

Positive correlated features? Negative? Impact on outcomes?
Positive correlated:
1. Overfitting: Including too many correlated variables may lead to overfitting, where the model mitigates the noise in the data rather than finding true underlying relationships between outcomes and features.
2. Multicollinearity: Including highly correlated variables can introduce multicollinearity. This can make it difficult to separate the individual effect of each variable on the outcome. Multicollinearity violates the assumption of the independence property of the variables.
Negative correlated:
1. Inverse Impact on the Outcome: An increase in one variable is associated with a decrease in the other. Consequently, the outcome variable is likely to be affected in the opposite direction.
2. Offset the multicollinearity: If two variables are highly negatively correlated, including both in the model (acting like a proxy variable for the positive multicolinearity) might stabilize the model and improve the estimation of the individual effects of each variable.
